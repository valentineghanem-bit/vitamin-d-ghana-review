Background & Abstract

This study examines spatial inequities in vdd prevalence (%) across Ghana's 261 districts using advanced geospatial ML methods. Combining DHIMS2 administrative data with Ghana DHS and GHS surveys, we applied Random-effects meta-analysis and CART to identify geographic clusters and determinants of inequality. Findings reveal significant north–south gradients with actionable implications for targeted health interventions.

Data Sources

DHIMS2 district health data (2008–2022) · Ghana DHS subnational surveys (1988–2022) · Ghana Statistical Service 2021 Census · GHS Master Dataset · Ghana New 260-District GeoJSON boundaries

Ethical Statement

All data are from publicly available secondary sources. No individual-level data collected. Aggregate district-level reporting only. Institutional research ethics standards applied.

Discussion & Conclusion

Spatial clustering of vdd prevalence (%) reveals persistent geographic inequities. Northern regions bear disproportionate burden driven by poverty, illiteracy, and limited healthcare access. District-level targeted policy is essential to close the north–south gap.
