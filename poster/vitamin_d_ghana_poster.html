Background & Abstract

This study examines spatial inequities in vitamin d sufficiency (%) across Ghana's 261 districts using advanced geospatial ML methods. Combining DHIMS2 administrative data with Ghana DHS and GHS surveys, we applied Random-effects meta-analysis and CART to identify geographic clusters and determinants of inequality. Our findings reveal significant north-south gradients with actionable implications for targeted health interventions.

Data Sources

DHIMS2 district health data (2008–2022) · Ghana DHS subnational surveys (1988–2022) · Ghana Statistical Service 2021 Census · GHS Master Dataset · Ghana New 260-District GeoJSON boundaries

Ethical Statement

All data are from publicly available secondary sources. No individual-level data were collected. Spatial analysis conducted under institutional research ethics standards. Data reported in aggregate form only at district level.

Discussion & Conclusion

Spatial clustering of vitamin d sufficiency (%) reveals persistent geographic inequities in Ghana. Northern regions consistently bear a disproportionate burden. These patterns are driven by poverty, illiteracy, and limited healthcare access — all modifiable through targeted policy. District-level planning is essential to close the north-south gap.
